# Under the Hood: How the Citation Graph Actually Gets Built

If you have read the [companion article](BEYOND_THE_BLUE_LINKS.md), you know the pitch: scientific literature is a network, not a list, and treating it that way has real practical value. This piece is about what happens when you actually try to build one of these things. It is a story of trade-offs, approximations, and the surprising number of places where you have to make a judgement call that meaningfully affects the result.

The whole pipeline has four stages: crawl the citation network, lay it out in 2D space, cluster it into communities, and label those communities so a human (or an agent) can make sense of them. Each stage throws away some information in exchange for tractability. The art, such as it is, is in choosing what to discard.

## Following the Threads

Everything starts with a handful of seed papers — in my original example case, five DOIs from the MRI-guided focused ultrasound thalamotomy literature. From there, the system does a breadth-first search: for each paper, it fetches both the papers it cites (references, via Crossref) and the papers that cite it (citers, via OpenAlex). Each of those newly discovered papers goes into the queue, and the process repeats.

![Breadth-First Search (BFS) expansion: seeds in the centre, the network radiating outward through successive hops](assets/fig-bfs-expansion.png)

One problem that I ran into initially was not respecting bidirectional crawl. References point backward in time — a 2023 paper citing a 2018 paper. Citers point forward — finding the 2025 papers that built on the 2023 one. If you only follow references, you get the history but miss the frontier. If you only follow citers, you get the latest work but lose the foundations it stands on.

Both Crossref and OpenAlex are free, open APIs - anyone with a laptop and a bit (probably a fairly large amount) of patience can build one of these graphs. Data collection is by a wide margin the slowest stage of the whole pipeline. The APIs have rate limits, and when you are fetching metadata for hundreds of thousands of papers one at a time, it adds up. Worse, the growth is exponential: five seeds became roughly 700,000 papers in four hops. You can control this somewhat by capping the number of citers fetched per paper — I default to 50 — but that is an explicit trade-off. You are deliberately discarding citing papers to keep the graph manageable, and there is no principled way to know whether the 51st citer was the one that mattered.

There are also gaps in the data itself. Some papers exist in Crossref but have zero references in their metadata — the publisher simply did not deposit them. Others have DOIs that resolve but return nothing useful. You learn to live with incompleteness. The graph is always a sample of the real citation network, never the whole thing, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

## Teaching a Computer to Draw

Once you have a graph — hundreds of thousands of nodes, millions of edges — you need to turn it into something a human can look at. The idea is borrowed from physics: treat every paper as a particle, every citation link as a spring connecting two particles, and then simulate the system. Connected nodes pull toward each other. Everything else pushes apart. Let it run long enough and the system settles into a layout where papers on similar topics end up near each other, because they share many of the same citation connections.

![Force-directed layout: before (chaos) and after (visible structure)](assets/fig-physics-layout.png)

The specific algorithm is ForceAtlas2, and for a graph this size, running it on a CPU is not realistic — it is fundamentally an N-body problem, and the naive version scales quadratically with the number of nodes. This is where the GPU comes in. RAPIDS cuGraph can run ForceAtlas2 on a graphics card, which turns what would be days of computation into minutes. The Barnes-Hut approximation helps too: instead of computing the repulsive force between every pair of nodes, it groups distant nodes together and treats them as a single mass. You are fudging the physics, but the error is small enough not to matter for visualisation purposes.

I should be honest about what you are getting out of this, though. The positions are meaningful — proximity really does encode topic similarity, and the broad spatial structure reflects the intellectual landscape of the field — but they are not sacred. Run the layout twice with different random seeds and you will get different positions. The overall shape will be similar (the same clusters will form) but the orientation will rotate, distances will shift. It is a useful simplification of a much higher-dimensional reality projected down into two dimensions so that a person can look at it. That is valuable, but it is not ground truth, and I think it is important not to oversell it.

The parameters matter more than I initially expected, too. Gravity controls how tightly everything pulls toward the centre — too high and you get a hairball, too low and the graph explodes into disconnected islands. Scaling ratio affects the spacing between clusters. I spent more time than I care to admit tweaking these numbers, and I am still not sure the values I settled on are optimal. They produce layouts that look right to me, but "looks right to me" is not exactly a rigorous criterion.

## Finding the Communities

With the graph laid out, the next step is to identify communities — groups of papers that are more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the network. I use the Louvain algorithm for this, mostly because it is fast, well-understood, and handles graphs at this scale without complaint.

Louvain works by optimising modularity — a measure of how well the network divides into groups where internal connections are dense and external connections are sparse. It is greedy: it starts with every node in its own community, then iteratively merges communities that improve the modularity score. It is also non-deterministic, which means running it twice can give you slightly different clusterings. In practice the broad structure is stable, but papers right on the boundary between two topics can go either way.

The real headache is the resolution parameter. It is a single number that controls the granularity of the clustering — how many communities you get and how large they are. Set it too low and you get a handful of enormous blobs that are too broad to be useful. Set it too high and you get hundreds of tiny fragments that are too specific to orient yourself. The correct value is domain-dependent, and there is no principled way to determine it without knowing something about the field you are mapping. I ended up picking a value that produced clusters that felt right for the thalamotomy literature, which is another instance of "looks right to me" masquerading as methodology.

![Hierarchical clustering: broad communities, with sub-communities inside](assets/fig-hierarchical-clustering.png)

The trick that helps a lot is running clustering twice — hierarchically. The first pass identifies broad thematic communities (neurosurgery, imaging physics, neurology, and so on). Then, within each of those communities, I extract the subgraph and run Louvain again to find finer-grained groupings. So "Focused Ultrasound Therapies" breaks down into sub-clusters for thalamotomy specifically, for other therapeutic targets, for device engineering, and so on. Two zoom levels turns out to be enough for both quick orientation ("what broad areas does this graph cover?") and meaningful exploration ("what are the sub-topics within neurosurgery?"). You could go deeper — clusters within clusters within clusters — but I have not found a practical need for a third level yet. I suspect if someone wanted to expand out further or even try the herculean task of mapping every paper ever, then 3, 4 or even more levels might well be necessary.

## The Hard Part: Giving Clusters Names

Clustering gives you numbered groups. Group 0 has 12,000 papers. Group 1 has 8,500. Group 2 has 30,000. This is useless without labels. The question is how to generate labels that are actually informative when you have potentially thousands of papers per cluster and an LLM that has a finite context window and a tendency to produce generic drivel if you give it too much to work with.

The naive approach is to dump every paper title in a cluster into the LLM prompt and ask it to summarise. This fails for several reasons. First, thousands of titles do not fit in a context window. Second, even if they did, the signal gets buried in noise — every cluster has papers that ended up there not because they are core to the topic but because they happen to share a few citation links with papers that are. A methodology paper from a completely different field that gets cited by three papers in your cluster will show up in the list and confuse the labelling.

The approach I landed on involves finding the most representative papers in each cluster before the LLM ever sees anything. The idea is straightforward: if you can identify which papers are most "central" to a cluster's topic, those are the ones the LLM should read.

![Centroid selection: the most representative papers (blue) are nearest the centre; outliers (grey) are filtered out](assets/fig-centroid-selection.png)

First, every paper's title and abstract gets encoded into a 768-dimensional vector using SapBERT — a language model fine-tuned on biomedical text, so it understands that "thalamotomy" and "ventral intermediate nucleus ablation" are essentially the same thing. Then, for each sub-cluster, I compute the centroid — the mean of all the embedding vectors, which you can think of as the mathematical "average paper." Finally, I select the 50 papers whose embeddings are closest to that centroid by cosine similarity. (*side note: if the concept of embedding space is new to you, the idea of this space is that each direction might be some kind of attribute or idea - for example a 'male' direction you'd expect that 'boy' would be (+1) and 'girl' (-1). If we add more directions (which is to say more dimensions of the vector), for example 'grown up', then boy might be (1, -1) while dad might be (1, 1). In practice we have basically no idea what each direction actually means, but we can still see if things are pointing in the same direction or not which is precisely what cosine similarity does.*)

What this does, in effect, is filter out the noise. Papers that ended up in the cluster because of a few coincidental citation links but are actually about something else will have embeddings far from the centroid — they are semantically distant from the cluster's centre of gravity. The 50 nearest papers, by contrast, are the ones most prototypical of whatever the cluster is actually about. The LLM sees a clean, coherent sample rather than a noisy dump of everything.

The labelling itself is bottom-up. Sub-clusters get labelled first: the LLM receives the titles of the 10 most representative papers plus a handful of TF-IDF keywords extracted from all 50, and produces a short (3–5 word) thematic label. (*Side note: TF-IDF stands for Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency. Term Frequency scores how often a word appears in a document; Inverse Document Frequency scores how rare it is across the collection. Words like "the" or "methods" that appear everywhere get a low score; distinctive terms get a high score. The combination highlights words that are characteristic of this particular cluster.*) Then parent cluster labels are generated by feeding the LLM the sub-cluster labels weighted by size — so the largest sub-topics dominate the parent name. It is a surprisingly effective way to build a human-readable taxonomy of a field without any human actually reading the papers.

I should be clear about the limitations. The labels are usually good but occasionally too generic — "Neuroimaging Methods" when something more specific would be appropriate. SapBERT is trained on biomedical text, so this whole approach assumes a biomedical domain; mapping a computer science citation network would need a different encoder. The LLM (Llama 3.1 8B, self-hosted) sometimes produces malformed output that needs parsing heuristics. Also, there's definitely better models out there which would be able to give more nuanced answers - I'm just too tight to pay for them for this. And 50 is a magic number that seems to work well but is not the result of any rigorous optimisation — I tried a few values and 50 consistently produced labels I was happy with. Principled? No. Functional? Yes.

## The Pipeline as a Whole

Each stage of this pipeline is a deliberate simplification. The crawl discards citers beyond a threshold. The layout projects a high-dimensional structure into two dimensions. The clustering depends on an arbitrary resolution parameter. The labelling samples 50 papers out of thousands. At every step, you are throwing away information to make the problem tractable.

The reason it works — and I think it does work, based on the [benchmark results](BEYOND_THE_BLUE_LINKS.md#putting-it-to-the-test) — is that there are some pragmatic choices that do lose information, but hopefully not information core to the functioning of the network. The citers beyond the 50th suggest that the original paper was quite impactful, potentially extending beyond the original neighbourhood. It's quite easy to stumble onto a hugely important paper and then have it dominate the map with all the ideas it spawns. Higher spatial dimensions would help separation but are not essential for orientation and, indeed, would be a nightmare to visualise. Having a method for trying to identify 'core' papers to a cluster does help to keep an LLM focused on producing something meaningful and clear. I (and you, if you wanted to) could remove these restrictions and have the perfect map — no limits on the search, crazy multi-dimensional space, the world's largest context window letting you feed 1,000 papers directly into Claude to ponder — but at the end of it, we're probably going to have a similarly functioning graph.

What I find most satisfying about the whole system is that it ends up as a queryable data structure, not just a picture. Every stage produces structured data — positions, cluster IDs, labels, edges — that gets loaded into PostgreSQL and exposed through an API. The same graph that helps a researcher visually orient themselves can help an AI agent programmatically decide where to look next. And because every step is explicit and inspectable, you can trace exactly why the system surfaced a particular paper, which is more than you can say for most AI-assisted search tools.
